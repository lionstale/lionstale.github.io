Taking the Perfect Picture

Written By: Shahriar Hossain

Back in the day, photography was something to specialize in; a camera was a rare and expensive tool that one would need to master. Nowadays, however, everyone has a camera in their pocket, but so few people actually know how to use it and take advantage of what it actually has to offer! Modern cameras are ridiculously easy to use, however as a result, it encourages a brainless point-and-shoot style of photography, and most people have forgotten the fundamentals of a good photo!

Lighting makes all the difference. Seriously. It’s what will determine the visibility, color, and perception of your photo.

Source of light: You need to know where your light is coming from so that you are able to use it to your advantage.

Harsh vs Soft light: Be aware that harsh lights will create very dark shadows, giving your photo lots of contrast, like the summer sun. On the other hand, soft lights will give you very even lighting, and may eliminate any shadows at all, such as a well-lit photo studio.

Color Temperature: Your light source will affect other colors in your photo. Take a fluorescent bulb; it’s very white, but it will not interfere or clash with other colors. However, an incandescent bulb will; it will cast a shade of yellow over your photo, something that you may need to correct.

"Lighting makes all the difference. Seriously."

Composition of your photo counts. This is what will determine what your viewer sees in the photo.

The Rule of thirds: This is a general rule of thumb. If you divide your photo into 9 equal parts, the cross sections of the dividends are where you want to place the subject or subjects of interest.

Focus: Where your photo is the sharpest is where your viewer’s eyes will wander first. Make it count.

Horizontal vs Vertical: Learn when it is appropriate to use either. If the subject of your photo is tall, say a tower, go vertical. If you are taking a large group photo, go horizontal. Of course, this completely depends on how you want to frame the picture for your subject(s).

Angle: Before you take a photo, explore your angles. You might find that certain elements of your subject are visible from one side, but not the other.

Finally, if you want to get serious, learn to post-process your photos. Personally, I use Pixlr, which is much like a free version of photoshop that can be used online. It can make a huge difference to your photos when you realize that there is still much that can be done even after you’ve taken your photo.

Do mind that this mini-guide does not outline what is right and what is wrong in photography. Everyone takes photos differently, and for different reasons. Some want to show their friends that they’re having a good time, and don’t care about the quality of their photo. Others take great pleasure in composing their shot and make every picture count. The creative controls are ultimately, up to you, and I can only hope that you give your next photos a little more thought after having read this article.
