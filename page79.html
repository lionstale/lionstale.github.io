Final Touches

Happy Valentine's Day!

Martine

Interviewed By Andreea Samareanu

“We met at the disco… Well, he was playing basketball, and I used to go to games because my father was a referee. That’s how I got to meet him. And you know… at the disco – you start to dance together, you see where it all leads, and voilà! – 37 years. So yeah, that’s the story, nothing much to it.”

Mme. Ruscher

Interviewed By Andreea Samareanu

“Well, it’s very simple. For my first job I was living in Strasbourg in France. One of my brothers already lived in Brussels – he went there for his studies and basically never left. At his job, there was this Finnish-Italian guy who he met and they got along very well. One weekend, I went to visit my brother because there was a jazz festival in Brussels. I had already been the year before and really enjoyed it. I had said, ‘Next year, I’m going to be back’, and I was. My brother and his girlfriend invited my future husband to join us for the first night. I was coming by train from France and they picked me up at the station. Well, he was there. And it was le ‘coup-de-foudre’. We were together that same weekend, and he asked me to marry him a week later.”

Mr. Jones

Interviewed By Andreea Samareanu

“It would have been one October – or was it November? – some thirty years ago. We were walking down the street and the leaves were falling – oak leaves, tea leaves, leaves the colour of faded pages from well-thumbed paperbacks whispering of a previous century. We had just been to Marcel’s, a charming little café near the World’s End, to have tea and some of those delightful little cakes with the French sounding name.

 

The street lights were humming out into the darkness although I didn’t recognise the tune. There was also a stir and bustle high up above our heads somewhere in the Crab Nebula. Out of the corner of my eye, I saw the pavement begin to rise in the direction of the sky and then I realised that they must be building an overpass.

 

My heart beat like a Kenwood Chefette Handmixer as her face came close to mine. She was white in the moonlight and resembled the Venus de Milo only with more clothes and a full complement of arms. I knew that when I kissed her, I would no longer be able to romp in my bedroom playing air guitar to Dark Side of the Moon. So, I waited to strike a fork against a tin of Carnation milk, but there were none to be found.

 

My lips parted like an IB student beginning their Individual Oral at the green light winking from the mp3 recorder across the table. But they made no sound and what I had almost remembered was uncommunicable … forever …

 

(Thomas Parke d’Invilliers)”
