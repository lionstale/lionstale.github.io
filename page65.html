Informal Tips for the Beginner/
Intermediate Guitarist 

Written By: Isaiah Lowney

In November of last year, I started playing guitar again. I was walking down the street, someone punched me really hard (in the head, mind you), and I, being the pacifist that I am, continued on my way to the store. But then something weird happened. Over the next few days, I started to gradually entertain the thought of playing the guitar. Eventually, after mounting cerebral pressure, I decided to pick one up and have a go at it. None of that is true, but hopefully it caught your attention, and made you aware of the dangers of hitting your head too hard (of which I am all too aware).


I had taken up drumming in the past few years, and having found out that the cool new kid played guitar, I went to jam with him in the music room a few times. Naturally, it was pretty rad, so we got a star pianist/drummer involved (also a cool kid, mind you), and our sessions happened more frequently.


I’d played guitar for a few years when I was in elementary school, but ultimately let it go. I’d first done a drum lesson, but soon dropped it because of my parents. 7-year-old me didn’t realize it wasn’t the lesson that was so expensive, but actually the drum set, until 17-year-old me looked at what a drum set costs. Anyways, it’s a good thing I switched to guitar, because I saved my parents a bit of money with my lazy, unmotivated attitude.


I’m writing this to shed light on my experience in learning to play the guitar using YouTube and guitar tab websites. I’m not saying guitar is for everyone, but for those who are motivated to play the riffs from their favorite album or impress girls with Wonderwall, it isn’t that much of a commitment. I’m no expert guitarist. This summer I went to a 3-day guitar camp, and it turns out I’m not quite as excellent as my mom says I am. But I still think that there’s a few tips I can pass on to beginner/intermediate guitarists that they might find helpful.

Tip #1: Strings

String gauge is how thick the strings are. The higher the gauge, the fatter the string. The fatter the strings, the better the tone. In more simple terms, for beginners like us, the higher the string gauge, the more shredded your hands will be. Not in a weird muscly way. More like you’ll rip your skin off slowly. Evidently, it really hurts. Stevie Ray Vaughan could play 13 gauge strings. You probably can’t. Oh, you say you can? That’s what I said, too. It hurt pretty bad, my tone wasn’t better, and I went from bad to worse. Turns out a bending vibrato isn’t that hard when you put on strings that match your playing ability. Also, people don’t know what string gauges are, and therefore won’t be at all impressed. Don’t limit yourself. Don’t be a wise guy like me and put on higher gauge strings. 

Tip #2: Music Theory

The second tip I have is to learn a bit of music theory. It seems useless (it did to me), but it isn’t. You can’t really write a good song by ear. And if you can understand the track you’re playing along to more deeply than which fret to hold, you’ll start to get what works and what doesn’t. Also, if you’re trying to become a really good guitarist, just know that no really good guitarist plays only covers. You’ll have to improvise solos and riffs. Sub-tip, don’t try to learn too much (unless you’re obsessed with music theory, in which case, knock yourself out), it’ll only bore you. An added bonus is that you can transcribe a song which the internet community hasn’t yet done, which is rare, but happens. 

Tip #3: Watch and Listen

My third tip is to watch and listen to pros play guitar, especially your favorites. Maybe watch a wannabe pro dissect how a pro plays on YouTube. Watching and listening is a good combination, because it makes you understand (roughly) what movements you need to do to obtain a certain sound. In order to first develop your own style as a guitarist, you need to look at the styles of other guitarists, and copy them. An original style is a fusion of influences. 

Tip #4: Play what you like

My final tip is to play what you like. If everything in this article went over your head, remember to just learn something you love and then play along to the song. Hearing your favorite song is pretty awesome, but hearing it come out of your own hands is perhaps even awesomer.


You might ask, why is this article not titled, “Why you should play the guitar”? It’s because I have no idea why you should play the guitar. Yes, a classical guitar can be well under 100 euros, and that’s nothing compared to how much high schoolers spend on their clothes these days, but I’ve found from my elementary school experience that it isn’t really worth your parents time and money to have you do something that you aren’t really into. You can wear your off-white jacket, but you can’t wear your gui…. Nevermind. You get the point. And in addition, with all the rap music and mac music that’s trending these days, there aren’t really many guitar legends who are captivating the mainstream audiences these days (T-Swizzle did okay but T-Swizzle is T-Swizzle). Not like they were in the 70s and 80s, anyways.


I can’t tell you to play the guitar, or that you need it in your life, or that it’ll change your life, or that it destresses you, or that it’ll get you girls or whatever. But if you play, you’ll find these tips might help you with a little troubleshooting as you progress. So have fun with playing guitar, or contemplating this article (or criticize it), and jazz on! Oh, and find some cool kids to play with, that’s like 99% crucial.
