Obverse Art Show

Art is an alchemy that transforms and changes the ordinary world into an extraordinary world. – Mr. Mitchell

The Obverse art showcase took place at the Greene Gallery on January 19th. The turnout was wonderful, “enriched with friends and colleagues, philosophy, art, and cake.”


The two visiting artists’ works each had many merits, but, as Mr. Graham, the MS and HS Theatre Teacher said, “These are two artists who have definitely found their voice, but those voices are very different.”

Audrey Atkinson


When asked about her inspiration, Audrey stated that most artists would say their inspiration is a dream, but her inspiration comes from anything, even a trash can on the street. To her, everything becomes beautiful or interesting. She likes the basis of art: the drawing. While she does sculpt, etch, and paint, she always makes sure to draw each day.


Her art style can change once in a while, but this is because she believes the world is constantly changing and one cannot do the same thing over and over. You have to push yourself to go further, as it must get boring to see the same thing all the time.


Some of her artwork was created on fabric such as silk, rather than paper. People often say that “you can’t do that,” because it is different, but Audrey dares to be different. She prefers to work on material that is light, transparent, and fluid – noble.


Some of her works are inspired by photos of poverty, ruin, and explosions. While to us appreciative viewers it seems a beautiful and magical thing, “it’s not really pretty if you know where the pictures come from.” This ability to create beauty from the darker parts of humanity is part of what makes Audrey’s work so special. One can choose to appreciate the art on a superficial level as being pleasing to the eye, or one can search deeper to see how darker inspiration has created a masterpiece.

Stephanie Wintjes


Stephanie was previously an art teacher at St. John’s, but is currently at art school. When she worked at the school, they used to have community shows, but this is her first real exhibition in The Greene Gallery and she is excited to be called a “visiting artist.”


Stephanie is fascinated by still life. She believes that it is a way to capture a dramatic moment that might otherwise be overlooked. Her inspiration for her recent work came from the work of a Spanish artist named Cotom. He painted dark cupboards with vegetables hanging on strings. This was how the Spanish people kept their produce separate to keep them from rotting. To her, this was truly magical to see these floating vegetables and she wished to capture the drama. Madame Froidcoeur, a HS Social Studies teacher was, “intrigued by the tension of the string holding something as massive as a vegetable,” in Stephanie’s work. Plus, she is “drawn to anything that has to do with food anyway.”


Some of her paintings focus on blue dish cloths, as to her the dish cloths were “posing” and she thought it would be interesting to capture an object people take for granted by finding the beauty in it. Another viewer saw the painting and said it look like a prison uniform or the “boy with the striped pajamas.” She had never seen it that way. This made her realize that for one person it could be an innocent dish towel, but to another it could be frightening. She realized that art is a political act which can make some people have an unbelievable interpretation of the seemingly innocent.


But, overall, she thinks that art should make you think and reflect so she is happy whenever someone finds an interpretation of her work that she had not even imagined.
